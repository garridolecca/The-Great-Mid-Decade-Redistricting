Redistricting is the process of redrawing congressional district boundaries. It typically happens every 10 years after the Census to reflect population changes.

Each district must contain roughly equal population (~760,000 people). How those lines are drawn determines which communities are grouped together and which party's voters have the majority.

Mid-decade redistricting — like what's happening in 2026 — is rare and deeply controversial.

Gerrymandering is the manipulation of district boundaries for political advantage. Named after Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry in 1812, it remains one of the most powerful tools in American politics.

The party that controls the state legislature typically draws the maps — and can draw them to entrench their own power for a decade.

Two core gerrymandering strategies:

Packing

Concentrate opposing voters into one district so they "waste" votes on an overwhelming victory.

Cracking

Split opposing voters across multiple districts so they never form a majority.

Redistricting normally happens after each decennial Census (2010, 2020, 2030). Mid-decade redistricting is rare — the last major one was Texas in 2003.

The 2026 cases are driven by political opportunity: parties that gained state-level power after 2024 elections are using that power to redraw maps before the next Census in 2030.

The Supreme Court ruled in Rucho v. Common Cause (2019) that federal courts cannot hear partisan gerrymandering claims. This leaves challenges to state courts, which have produced contradictory results.

North Carolina's state supreme court reversed its own precedent in just 2 years after its partisan composition changed.

In an unusual move for 2026, several U.S. states are redrawing their congressional maps mid-decade. This interactive map visualizes how redistricting reshapes political representation at every scale — from statewide overviews down to individual streets.

Why it matters A single street-level change in a district boundary can flip a district's political leaning, affecting representation for hundreds of thousands of people.
